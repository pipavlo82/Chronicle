# Receipts as Assets

## Why verified history may have value

Verified history can matter because it answers questions that markets, institutions, and collaborators care about:

- What was actually done?
- Under what constraints?
- With what evidence?
- What was verified independently?
- How does this work relate to prior work?

The stronger and more portable those answers become, the more economically useful the receipts may be.

## Potential sources of value

A receipt may derive value from:

- proof of authorship or stewardship
- proof of contribution to a larger mission
- proof of participation in a historically important event
- durable engineering or research track record
- replayable evidence for future audits
- composability into larger verified portfolios
- downstream settlement or royalty references

## Why the receipt may be the asset

Traditional digital asset systems often separate the asset from the evidence around it.
Chronicle asks whether that split is backwards.

If the enduring thing is the verifiable history, then the receipt is not metadata attached to value. The receipt is the carrier of value.

## Not every receipt is valuable

Chronicle does not assume universal value. Most receipts may remain utilitarian.
But some receipts may accumulate value because they sit at important intersections of:

- work
- verification
- provenance
- reuse
- historical relevance

That is enough to justify treating receipts as a candidate asset class.
